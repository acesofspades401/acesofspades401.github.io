What is the ACS?

The ACS is a short and simple way to get an overview of a specific anomaly. It is designed to be as clear and concise as possible, as to reduce the risk of misidentification. It is broken down into 4 segments with 6 data points total, which give enough information about an anomaly to allow for personnel to safely approach the anomaly without any worry that they are unprepared.

Breakdown of the ACS

On an SCP document, the ACS is utilized in the document header and it is formatted as shown below:

Anomaly Identification:SCP-NG-000
Clearance Level:0 - Public Information
Containment Class: Undefined
Secondary Class: None
Disruption Class: 0/none
Risk Class: 0/none

Top row

On the top most row, there are two elements: The anomalies identification, and the clearance level for the related SCP file. The identification's nomenclature is described here. Next to the identification comes the SCP's file clearance level. This means that any personnel looking to read up more on the anomaly must ensure they have the proper clearance level to do so. More on clearance levels can be found here.

Bottom Row

The bottom row contains four elements. These can get a bit more complex as it describes an anomalies containment class, secondary class, disruption class, and risk class. Lets break these down two by two.

Containment and secondary class

An anomalies containment class and secondary class defines an anomalies containment resistance and additional anomalous properties (e.g. cognitohazard, esoteric, apollyon, etc.).

The primary classes are listed below:

  • Explained – An anomaly that is explainable with current scientific understandings. Its anomalous effects are deemed non-anomalous, and the anomaly is typically neutralized after some time if no future use can be found for it
  • Neutralized – An anomaly that has been deemed either explained and useless, possess such a great threat that the Foundation had no choice but to neutralize (destroy) it, or is in a state where containment is not worth the burden and thus requires neutralization.
  • Safe – Safe anomalies are anomalies that either possess anomalous properties that pose no real risk, but still require some form of safeguarding from the public, or are easily contained with minimal containment requirements (i.e throw it in a locked box and check up on it every now and then).
  • Euclid – These anomalies are harder to contain. They typically aren't inherently trying to evade containment, but they are troublesome to contain. For example: a semi-sentient goo would most likely be Euclid, as it doesn't inherently try to escape containment, but its nature is that it likes to move, and thus requires stricter containment requirements.
  • Keter – Keter anomalies are difficult to contain, possess moderate to severe anomalous effects and most likely actively will try to evade containment. These anomalies typically are sentient or large-scale, and strict protocols are often developed on a case-by-case basis for them.
Note

The secondary classes are more esoteric and specific, so they will not be listed in this document, rather they will be updated periodically in an appendix at the bottom of this document.

Disruption class and risk class

An anomalies disruption class refers to the amount the anomaly disrupts the status quo, causes panic, or otherwise causes a shift in the balance of normalcy. It is broken down into 5 levels described below:

  1. Discomfort – The anomaly would cause a general discomfort to the average person and would cause a minor disruption to the status quo
  2. Unease – The anomaly would cause a greater feeling of unease to someone and would cause a notable disruption to the status quo
  3. Doubt – The anomaly would actively cause panic in a person and disrupt the status quo to a point where people would begin to question reality.
  4. Disbelief – The anomaly would cause such a panic and disrupt the status quo to a point where the Foundation would have a hard time to repair it
  5. Shock – The anomaly would cause irreversible panic, and threaten not only the status quo, but possibly the future of the world as we know it

An anomalies risk class states the severity of an anomalies effects on a subject or society, and gives an understanding on how damaging and permanent the effects are. It is broken down into 5 levels as-well.

  1. Notice – The anomalies effects are typically either short-lasting, unnoticeable, or otherwise minimal in damage to a subject or object.
  2. Caution – The anomalies effects display moderately, last a decent amount of time or can cause concerning amounts of damage
  3. Warning – The anomalies effects display severely and obviously, last a long time, or can cause severe amount of damage
  4. Danger – The anomalies effects extend beyond a single subject or object, last indefinitely, and typically cause irreversible damage
  5. Critical – The anomalies effects extend to a broad area, last indefinitely, and cause irreversible damage

Appendix A – Secondary Containment Classes

This is a working list of Secondary Containment Classes in the ACS

  • Theo – Theoretical: This class of anomalies is theoretical and may or may not exist. Evidence supporting the existence is present, but not strong enough to convince the Foundation of any imminent threat to the status quo.
